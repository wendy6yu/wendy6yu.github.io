CIAO CHOW!

Deciding Where to Eat in Less Than 5 Minutes

DATE
March 2020

TEAM
Rosa Weinberg (Mentor)
This past week in EngSci-22: Designing for Desirability, we were assigned a 1-week-long design challenge in which we were asked to a) conceptualize a product that targeted and modified a specific behavior using Cialdini’s and/or Fogg’s theories on human behavior and b) to create a user experience journey map as our deliverable.

My group envisioned an app that targeted decision fatigue associated with finding and picking restaurants by gamifying the experience through a Tinder-esque swipe UI that was easily filterable and reduced the often text-heavy interfaces of restaurant apps to a majority visual interface.

While my group decided to focus on an experience targeted towards the individual, as I was at office hours working through product details with my TF (big, big, shoutout to Rosa Weinberg for her time and advice), we came up with a branching idea that was so much fun that I needed to write it down as a formal proposal. That said, here it is below!

THE CHALLENGE

My target behavioral problem was more specifically as follows: the indecision in deciding where to eat that occurs in group settings.



In order to help groups make eating decisions more efficiently, I first needed to understand why this indecision occured. So why is choosing a restaurant so difficult for groups? I boiled it down to three major categories:
  • 1/ Taking responsibility for your own happiness is one thing, but being responsible for others’ happiness is an entirely separate problem. Scary.
  • 2/ There are So. Many. Choices. This indecision-increasing factor applies to both individuals and groups. There are really just so many options for food, and while we would generally agree that optionality a privilege, humans don’t always seem to do too well with it.
  • 3/ Am I going to spend my time and energy Yelping, or are you? Yelp is wonderful and I use it all the time, but it is also a dense and energy-draining website. People are lazy (or maybe just tired?), and everyone wants to delegate the research to someone else.

SOLUTION

To resolve this indecision, Ciao Chow! aims to do the following:
  • 1/ It incorporates all members in the decision-making process, and therefore divides up the responsibility between the group so that the pressure is reduced for every individual.
  • 2/ It gives the group a limited number of choices, because in the end having 10,000 options on Yelp v. having 20 options on this app is not really going to affect anyone's meal utility [in terms of satisfaction gained]. As the age-old adage goes, “You can’t miss what you never had."
  • 3/ It makes the entire process less energy-draining by employing a gamified, visual interface that encourages users to make quick choices based on visceral reactions. As the other age-old adage goes, “A picture is worth 1000 words.”
So how does this app achieve the goals above? I maintained the swipe interface that my team used for our restaurant app in the design challenge, but modified it so that all members of a dinner party can enter into a group. The app then presents a set of filters, which breaks down the decision making process through straightforward questions that are easy to answer. Afterwards, each member is individually shown a set of restaurant cards on their app, which they swipe through on their own time (it should only take a few minutes maximum for an individual). When all members of the group finish swiping, the group is shown the cards that all members have swiped right on or “liked.” In this manner, the app cuts the number of restaurant options down significantly and guarantees that the final selection is one that maximizes that satisfaction of all group members.

CONCLUSION

Decision fatigue plagues both individuals and groups, but can be resolved or at least minimized by the effective use of technology and the provision of certain givens or constraints.

While this app is designed to reduce the mental strain of nagivating the complex world of group decisions, there are a number of further considerations to work through, e.g.: At what point does creating and swiping through these groups actually become more work than choosing a restaurant? What are the bounds of too much and too little information to show on each restaurant card?

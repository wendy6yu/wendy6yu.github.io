COPYSMITH

Boosting content marketing with the magic of AI

DATE
July — Dec 2020

TEAM
Ray Inoue (Product Designer)
Kamila Krauze (Springboard)
The existing universe of marketing copy is infinitely large, impossible to navigate, and composed of Google Sheet mazes, hours upon hours of SEO research, and chains of emails between writers, managers, and creative directors.

This is where Copysmith comes in as the figurative Cat in the Hat cleaning machine — without giving away too much, we entered this sphere with the intention of using AI to eliminate time spent composing marketing copy, although you'll see that much has changed since then.

I joined Copysmith summer of 2020 as their first and initially sole designer (eventually we onboarded another product designer as well as several short term designers from Springboard). At the time, I first joined to design Copysmith's branding and general design system, after which I transitioned into the position of lead product designer and spearheaded the development of MVP and its later iterations. While building the MVP, I also worked closely with our growth and engineering teams.

We launched our MVP October 26, 2020 on Product Hunt, and ended as the #2 Product of the Day and have since amassed over 1200 upvotes.

This case study is focused on MVP development, for which we had 3 primary goals:
  • 1/ Our tool should first and foremost quickly generate quality copy for users. This meant minimal input on user side.
  • 2/ Some degree of flexibility should be embedded in the interface — we decided users would strongly desire the ability to sort through and refine copy.
  • 3/ Copy should be stored on an organized, central hub for easy access.
Below, I'll do a deeper dive into each of these goals and their associated problems.

PROBLEM DEFINITION

This was definitely a non-linear path, and is frankly still evolving. If we backtrack to the very beginning, our team initially envisioned Copysmith as a long-form content generator because we knew that businesses with significant online traffic needed to generate hundreds of blog posts a month, which we imagined to be hugely time consuming.



To test out this idea, Copysmith had a pre-MVP product which consisted solely of a somewhat lengthy Typeform which the user would complete — we would then feed their answers into the AI and have it return a blog post, which we would email in plain HTML to the user. As we began speaking to users, however, we quickly ran into 2 realizations:
  • 1/ It is VERY difficult for users to trust AI-generated long-form content. Post-generation, they will probably still comb through and edit the text quite thoroughly, therefore limiting Copysmith's usefulness.
  • 2/ There is a whole world of short form copy that needs to be generated. This copy is also far more straightforward for our AI to generate.

MVP - HIGH FIDELITY PROTOTYPE

Omitting the intermediary drafts and trial, our completed MVP was a simple set of screens driven by the singular goal of generating original, quality short form content with minimal work for the user.

Creating copy only took 3 steps:

1/ Navigate to desired copy type, and open new copy template.
2/ Complete the required fields.
3/ Hit generate, and browse through copy types. We provided 10 variations with each "Generate" button click.
We also provided users with a number of ways to interact with their copy post-generation as follows:



Like. Liked generations would bubble to the top of the generations list for easier access.


Delete. Gone forever!


Copy. Generations could be copied on click.


Lock. Generations were locked on default - unlocking allowed users to edit the copy.


The same process worked for our other content templates, which at the time also included Google Ads, Facebook ads, and taglines.

BACK TO THE DRAWING BOARD

Having an MVP was crucial for understanding many things about Copysmith's future that remained hidden otherwise.

Post-launch, our team began organizing feedback immediately. We sourced user input through several means, including:
  • 1/ Direct user testing
  • 2/ User Onboarding: we personally onboarded many of our first users both to alleviate the confusion of using a new interface and to gauge customer understanding.
  • 3/ Hotjar
  • 4/ Community Slack
We received a range of feedback, which I sorted first by micro vs. macro (micro referring to small, independent interface changes - generally quick to implement and not affecting the core Copysmith product vision, and macro being changes that would significantly affect either the Copysmith audience or product goal and purpose), and then by the below categories:



Within this section, I'll discuss the feedback given for micro changes, and leave macro changes for the "Next Steps" section. On the micro end, our user feedback was filtered and assembled into this affinity diagram:



More difficult than sourcing feedback was sorting through the feedback and prioritizing changes, especially given limited engineering resources and time.

Some of the issues were quick to resolve: click to copy became click to edit and drag over text to copy, which allowed us to eliminate the troublesome lock UI. We received many requests for a CSV download, so we also implemented that function. Based off of the quantity of similar feedback, we also deemed some sort of share ability and certain changes to our input form necessary and relatively easy to implement — these I'll detail in the following section.

Finally, some of the feedback we felt were reasonable changes, but would be longer term endeavors — in particular, we decided we needed to improve our dashboard architecture and in-file generation organization, but are still in the process of designing and implementing these changes.

MVP 1.5

This is where our product is currently at, although certainly not where it will stay at for long. Improved since MVP 1 are the following:

1/ We updated the audience and brand keywords UI to resolve the limiting column format. We also added explanatory tooltips next to fields that users found confusing to fill out.

2/ We designed an onboarding process, during which users would be able to fill out essential info used later to autopopulate templates.

3/ We shipped a share function, which produced a URL with the generated copy in list format. We also added an upvote/downvote system to this page to maximize team collaboration.

4/ Finally, a sneak preview at an upgraded dashboard, still very much a work in progress.

NEXT STEPS

It's been incredibly important to sync with other teams consistently both to see if the smaller pieces we're working on align but also to confirm that we agree on the bigger picture of Copysmith at hand.

We recently teamed up with Iain Thomas, an author and also creative director well versed in the marketing world, to develop our product strategy. Our latest meeting focused on product market fit and recalibrating our direction, because we realized we had amassed a considerable user base in the past few weeks that was pulling Copysmith towards different futures.

Specifically, what Copysmith might look like if we target SMEs v. entrepreneurs v. B2Bs v. agencies v. Shopify stores varies quite dramatically. Our analytics indicated that product descriptions were our most highly used content template by far, suggesting that Copysmith could be quite successful as a companion app to Shopify (read: focus on integrations).

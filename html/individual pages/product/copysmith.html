COPYSMITH

Boosting content marketing with the magic of AI

DATE
July 2020—Now

TEAM
Ray Inoue
Jenny Zhang



The existing universe of marketing copy is infinitely large, impossible to navigate, and composed of spreadsheet mazes, hours of SEO research, and record-breaking long email chains between writers, managers, and creative directors.

This is where Copysmith comes in as the figurative Cat in the Hat cleaning machine — without giving away too much,
we entered this sphere with the intention of using AI to eliminate time spent composing marketing copy
, although you'll see that much has changed since then.

My role:
I joined Copysmith summer of 2020 as their first and initially sole designer (eventually we onboarded another product designer as well as several short term designers from Springboard). At the time, I first joined to design Copysmith's branding, after which I transitioned into the position of Design Lead and spearheaded the development of the MVP and its later iterations. While building the MVP, I also worked closely with our growth and engineering teams.

We launched our MVP October 26, 2020 on Product Hunt, and ended as the #2 Product of the Day and are at $23K+ MRR 2 months later. Cheers to an even stronger 2021!


This case study is focused on MVP development, for which we had 3 primary goals:
  • 1/ Our tool should first and foremost quickly generate quality copy for users. This meant minimal input on user side.
  • 2/ We were realistic in our expectations for the quality of AI-generated copy (at least at MVP level); therefore, we needed to a) provide users with enough copy to guarantee several good results and b) allow for flexible editing of copy.
  • 3/ Copy should be stored on an organized, central hub for easy access.
Below, I'll do a deeper dive into each of these goals and their associated problems.

PROBLEM DEFINITION

This was definitely a non-linear path, and is frankly still evolving. Our team
initially envisioned Copysmith as a long-form content generator
because we knew that businesses with significant online traffic needed to generate hundreds of blog posts a month, which we imagined to be hugely time consuming.





Specifically, we received feedback along the lines of the following:

"We currently spend 4-6 hours researching and writing a blog post, and if Copysmith can at least [halve] that, it's a game changer."

To test out our potential in this market, Copysmith had an MVP 0.0 which consisted solely of a Typeform which the user would complete — we would then send their answers to OpenAI's API and return the generated blog post in plain HTML to the user via email.




As we began speaking to users, however, we quickly ran into 2 realizations:
  • 1/ It is VERY difficult for users to trust AI-generated long-form content off the bat. Post-generation, they will probably still comb through and edit the text quite thoroughly, therefore limiting Copysmith's usefulness.
  • 2/ There is a whole world of short form copy that needs to be generated. This copy is also far more straightforward to generate.

Some other important questions we asked during initial research included:
  • 1/ Which forms of copy are most valuable to our customers to generate? Internally, we had to work with engineering to determine what types of content could be generated as well as the necessary parameters for each.
  • 2/ What are the minimum and maximum bounds of how much input we can ask from the user, and what is the ideal balance? (Measured primarily in terms of time, but also in terms of mental energy required by different types of form fields.) This required an understanding of what our users valued more between efficiency and quality.

The majority of our questions, however, were difficult to answer without some sort of MVP, so we quickly moved to prototyping and development.

MVP - HIGH FIDELITY PROTOTYPE

Omitting the intermediary drafts, our completed MVP was a simple set of screens
driven by the singular goal of generating original, quality short form content with minimal work for the user
.

Creating copy only took 3 steps:


1/ Navigate to desired copy type, and open new copy template.
2/ Complete the required fields.
3/ Hit generate, and browse through copy types. We provided 10 variations with each "Generate" button click.

We also provided users with a number of ways to interact with their copy post-generation as follows:




Like. Liked generations would bubble to the top of the generations list for easier access.


Delete. Gone forever!


Copy. Generations could be copied on click.


Lock. Generations were locked on default - unlocking allowed users to edit the copy.



The same process worked for our other content templates, which at the time also included Google Ads, Facebook ads, and taglines.


BACK TO THE DRAWING BOARD

Having an MVP was crucial for understanding many things about Copysmith's future that remained hidden otherwise.

Post-launch, our team began organizing feedback immediately.
We sourced user input through several means, including:
  • 1/ Direct user testing
  • 2/ User Onboarding: we personally onboarded many of our first users both to alleviate the confusion of using a new interface and to gauge customer understanding.
  • 3/ Hotjar, and later Clarity
  • 4/ Community Slack

We received a range of feedback, which I sorted first by micro vs. macro (micro referring to small, independent interface changes - generally quick to implement and not affecting the core Copysmith product vision, and macro being changes that would significantly affect either the Copysmith audience or product goal and purpose), and then by the below categories:





Within this section, I'll discuss the feedback given for micro changes, and leave macro changes for the "Next Steps" section.
On the micro end, our user feedback was filtered and assembled into this affinity diagram
(click to enlarge):





The greatest challenge here was prioritizing feedback, especially given limited engineering resources and time.

Some of the issues were quick to resolve: click to copy became click to edit and drag over text to copy, which allowed us to eliminate the troublesome lock UI. Prioritizing by number of similar feedback and ease of engineering, we also implemented sharing copy, CSV downloads, and auditing and updating out input fields (more details in the following section).

Finally, some of the feedback we felt were reasonable changes, but would be longer term endeavors. The biggest needle mover for users, of course, was always generation quality and variety of copy templates (we had many requests for new copy types ranging from social media captions to Q&A's).
On the design end, we decided our big picture method of making the user journey more efficient and enjoyable had 3 paths:
  • 1/ Improve organization, both in terms of dashboard architecture and in-file generation organization (folders, presets, tagging and filtering, etc.).
  • 2/ Reduce time to generation (autopopulated fields, word banks/suggestions, presets, bulk importing, etc.).
  • 3/ Flesh out "next steps" post-generation — i.e. "What can I do with this generation, and how do I best get it to my final destination?" (improve sharing, exporting, integrations, etc.)

These, however, are longer term changes made as a part of the journey of acheiving product market fit. First, we'll discuss some smaller changes that have already been implemented.

MVP 1.5—2

MVP 1.5 is where our product is currently at, although certainly not where it will stay at for long. Improved since MVP 1 are the following:

1/ We updated the audience and brand keywords UI to resolve the limiting column format. We also added explanatory tooltips next to fields that users found confusing to fill out.


2/ We designed an onboarding process, during which users would be able to fill out essential info used later to autopopulate templates.


3/ We shipped a share function, which produced a URL with the generated copy in list format. We also added an upvote/downvote system to this page to maximize team collaboration.


4/ Of course, we also shipped a number of new templates, including Instagram Ads, landing pages, blog ideas, and a content expander. We average almost 1 new template a week, which is exciting both for us and for the users!
For MVP 2, we have some exciting (and hefty!) changes.


1/ Jenny Zhang and I are overhauling the design system. We just finished an audit of our existing one, which means in February, the Copysmith you see is likely to look quite different!


2/ After much designing, usability testing, and re-designing, I'm closing in on a new design for the dashboard that allows for folder hierarchy and presets to solve issues of organization and time to generation.


3/ Finally, we've created "Export as CSV" for bulk exporting, but have yet to tackle bulk importing. This, however, is coming for MVP 2!

NEXT STEPS

It's been incredibly important to sync with other teams consistently both to see if the smaller pieces we're working on align but also to confirm that we agree on the bigger picture of Copysmith at hand.

We recently teamed up with Iain Thomas, an author and also creative director well versed in the marketing world, to develop our product strategy.
Our latest meeting focused on product market fit and recalibrating our direction, because we realized we had amassed a considerable user base in the past few weeks that was pulling Copysmith towards different futures.

Specifically, what Copysmith might look like if we target SMEs v. entrepreneurs v. B2Bs v. agencies v. Shopify stores varies quite dramatically.
Our analytics indicated that product descriptions were our most highly used content template by far, suggesting that Copysmith could be quite successful as a companion app to Shopify (read: focus on plugins).

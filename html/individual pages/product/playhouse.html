PLAYHOUSE

Returning a 3rd Dimension to Virtual Theater and Performance

DATE
Oct — Nov 2020

TEAM
Nicole Araya
Cindy Wang
Wittman Goh
PlayHouse is an early-stage startup currently housed at the Harvard Innovation Labs. The PlayHouse app focuses on bringing a 3rd dimension of entertainment and interaction to what has now become 2D virtual performance.

My role:
This fall, the Harvard Design Collective (Harvard's first organization for digital creatives, founded by 5 wonderful women and myself) took on PlayHouse as one of our clients. I served as design lead for this project, and worked closely with several of our agency's designers to define the scope of their product, design the architecture of their app, and prototype a few of the screens.

Our project cycle finished in November, and PlayHouse is currently in development stage.

This case study is focused on PlayHouse's general product ideation and on the design of the dashboard section of MVP.

PROBLEM DEFINITION

The PlayHouse initiative began as a web app intended to stream performances and provide interactive programs that would bring a missing sense of fun to virtual theater. Our clients quickly ran into a number of legal and partnership issues regarding streaming, however, so it became necessary to pivot the central purpose of their product.

Rather than replacing existing streaming services, our team focused on designing PlayHouse as a companion app.


Our clients had already defined their audience to be frequent performance (especially theater) attendees, and had conducted early interviews around the idea of Playhouse. After speaking closely with the founders and understanding their vision for the app and pain points they had noted from these interviews,
we realized that they had two problems in focus:
  • 1/ Users found it frustrating to need to navigate from website to website to discover shows, acquire information, and book tickets from each different venue.
  • 2/ Post-transition to streamed performances due to Covid, many theater enthusiasts were disappointed with the virtual experience and missed the feeling and excitement of going to a physical venue.

DESIGN PROCESS

With respect to the two problems we defined above, my team proposed the below solution to each:
  • 1/ Allow users to curate their own playhouse — a collection of concert and theater venues for easy, centralized access to information from different places.
  • 2/ Design fun, interactive programs that users can browse as they livestream shows, allowing a more in depth understanding of and connection with the show than a plain text PDF might achieve.

Our thoughts: There has to be something better than this!



The interactive program could be tackled as a relatively independent component, so we temporarily set that aside and focused on how we would aggregate and organize venue information while maintaining PlayHouse's fun and experimental core branding.
We divided the app into 3 levels of curation:
  • 1/ A "Saved" tab: This would be the most user-curated tab — individual shows handpicked by the user, falling into the categories of either "Saved for the future" or "Purchased ticket to."
  • 2/ An "Explore" tab: This would be the least user-curated tab — shows and venues suggested by the app, albeit based loosely off of past user behavior.
  • 3/ The user's dashboard, or "Playhouse": This was intended to fall between the previous 2 in terms of user-curation. Venues would be added personally by the user; however, the performances shown in each venue would be ones selected by the venue themselves (through a separate internal interface).

HIGH FIDELITY PROTOTYPE

All primary information and activity is connected to the user's dashboard in the PlayHouse app. This was designed as a digital literal play "house," or a building designed by the user to house all preferred venues. While we brainstormed a number of different methods of representing distinct venues and spaces, our team ended up moving forward with the idea of a floorplan, which already carries a familiar association with space and buildings in particular.

Users can interact with their dashboard in 3 different ways:

1/ Users can
browse their existing playhouse for venue updates
(primarily to check if new shows have been added, represented by a gem icon). Each venue block, when tapped, brings up a page with venue information, updated internally by the venues themselves.
2/ Users can edit their playhouse —
floors and blocks can be deleted, moved, or updated
in both floorplan view and list view.
3/ From editing mode, users can also
add either a venue or floor
. A rectangular block representing a venue can be customized and added to any floor, or a floor (a grouping of venues) can be added to the entire PlayHouse.


An additional feature we had discussed with the client was the possibility of rendering the playhouse in 3D upon horizontal phone orientation.



However, while this was and remains an exciting creative idea, we removed this feature for several reasons, including:
  • 1/ What did the 3D interface bring to the table that the 2D interface didn't already have? We imagined the 3D interface to be a fun, gamified experience — for example, users might treasure hunt for new shows or other hidden features, but found that we couldn't otherwise justify the additional user confusion this would cause.
  • 2/ Practically speaking, this would require engineering expertise that simply was not available at the time.

Finally, we also prototyped a live chat screen and an interactive program for PlayHouse in Figma.

REFLECTION

In general, this app dealt with a topic very close to my interests — that concerning the increasing transition of physical spaces and interactions to digital forms and means. We were constantly asking ourselves questions such as, "What do the users miss most about actually going to venues? What are existing problems of physical venues? What are familiar indicators and symbols found in venues that audience members might find either useful or comforting? What are other physical things or spaces that have transitioned well to digital?" etc.

That being said, because I am so interested in this at an academic level, it was easy to get carried away and often necessary to reel myself back in for a reality check on how practical and usable the app was.

Finally, this was a project with a relatively condensed timeline and a very widely spread team (we had a team member across the ocean in Singapore!). All members of the design team were also simultaneously occupied full-time either as a student or intern, meaning efficient and consistent communication ended up being extremely vital to project success. Despite the unprecedented circumstances, however, I am insanely grateful to have worked with a machine of a team — will definitely miss our rose-bud-thorn check-ins at each meeting and Figma cursor-hovering!
